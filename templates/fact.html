Food Waste Crisis at IIT Guwahati

Food waste in IIT Guwahati's hostel messes, particularly at Kapili Hostel, is a significant issue with environmental and financial implications. Addressing this problem is crucial for promoting sustainability and aligning with global efforts to reduce food waste.

Key Statistics on Food Waste

  • Fact 1: In India, up to 33.33% of all food produced goes uneaten, with 95% of discarded food ending up in landfills.
  • Fact 2: India wastes about 67 million tonnes of food annually, equivalent to the food consumption of the state of Bihar.
  • Fact 3: At Kapili Hostel, an average of 59.57 kg of food is wasted at breakfast, 68.14 kg at lunch, and 65.71 kg at dinner daily.
  • Fact 4: India ranks 101st out of 116 countries in the Global Hunger Index 2021, with 189.2 million undernourished people.
  • Fact 5: Food waste contributes to methane emissions and increases the carbon footprint, making it a critical environmental concern.

Why Food Waste Management Matters

Reducing food waste at IIT Guwahati not only addresses environmental concerns but also tackles financial strain and supports food security. By implementing innovative solutions like pre-booking meals, using RFID cards, and optimizing menus, we can foster a culture of responsibility and sustainability.

Proposed Solutions

  • Pre-Booking: A website for students to pre-book meals, notify kitchen staff of quantities needed, and automate refunds for skipped meals.
  • RFID Cards: Track meal attendance and impose penalties for habitual meal skipping to promote accountability.
  • Self-Throwing: Install bins with weighing machines to encourage students to monitor and reduce their plate wastage.
  • Small Serving: Use smaller utensils, train staff on portion control, and offer tasting portions to minimize overserving.
  • Menu Optimization & Reusing: Use seasonal ingredients, repurpose leftovers through alternative cooking techniques, and extend shelf life via pickling and fermenting.
  • Biodegradation: Convert food waste into biogas, fertilizers, and enzymes to create renewable energy and high-value products.

Just-in-Time (JIT) Inventory Model

The JIT model, inspired by the Toyota Production System, minimizes inventory by preparing food based on pre-booked orders. This reduces waste, improves food quality, and ensures efficient use of resources. Benefits include lower costs, better cash flow, and smoother production cycles.
